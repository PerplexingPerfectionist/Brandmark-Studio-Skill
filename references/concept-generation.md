# Concept Generation

## Visual Metaphor Creation

A visual metaphor translates abstract brand values into concrete, recognizable symbols. The goal is to find a symbol that maps to the brand's essence in a way that is both intuitive and memorable.

### Metaphor Mapping Process

1. **Extract brand values**: List the core values, personality traits, and promises of the brand.
2. **Find concrete symbols**: For each value, brainstorm 3-5 concrete objects, animals, shapes, or natural phenomena that embody that value.
3. **Test for relevance**: Does the symbol make sense in the brand's industry? Would the audience understand the connection?
4. **Test for distinctiveness**: Is this symbol overused in the category? Can you twist it?
5. **Test for visual potential**: Does the symbol have a strong silhouette? Can it be simplified to an icon? Does it work in negative space?

### Metaphor Categories

| Category | Examples | Use When |
|----------|----------|----------|
| **Animals** | Lion (strength), owl (wisdom), eagle (vision), fox (cleverness), bear (protection), deer (grace), wolf (loyalty), raven (mystery) | Brand has a clear personality trait that maps to an animal. Archetypes: Hero, Sage, Explorer, Outlaw. |
| **Natural elements** | Mountain (achievement), tree (growth), wave (flow), flame (passion), leaf (sustainability), star (excellence), sun (energy), moon (mystery) | Brand connects to nature, sustainability, or elemental qualities. Archetypes: Explorer, Innocent, Magician. |
| **Objects / tools** | Compass (direction), key (access), shield (protection), lamp (insight), crown (authority), bridge (connection), anchor (stability), rocket (ambition) | Brand has a functional promise or solves a specific problem. Archetypes: Ruler, Hero, Caregiver, Sage. |
| **Abstract forms** | Spiral (growth), triangle (stability), circle (unity), square (structure), arrow (progress), wave (energy), star (spark), hexagon (connection) | Brand is abstract, tech-focused, or modern. Archetypes: Creator, Magician, Explorer. |
| **Body parts / human** | Eye (vision), hand (service), heart (passion), head (intelligence), foot (journey), wing (freedom), smile (joy) | Brand is human-centered, personal, or emotional. Archetypes: Caregiver, Lover, Jester, Innocent. |
| **Geometric combinations** | Interlocking shapes (connection), overlapping forms (transparency), negative space (hidden meaning), fractal patterns (complexity), grid (order) | Brand is about systems, technology, or complex ideas. Archetypes: Sage, Creator, Ruler. |

### Avoiding Clichés

Before selecting a symbol, check if it's overused in the category:

- **Technology**: Lightbulbs (ideas), clouds (cloud computing), gears (settings), circuit boards (tech), rockets (startups), infinity loops (endless).
- **Finance**: Columns (banks), scales (justice/balance), upward arrows (growth), piggy banks (savings), shields (security).
- **Health**: Crosses (medical), hearts (care), leaves (natural), hands (healing), snakes (pharmacy).
- **Education**: Books (learning), graduation caps (achievement), owls (wisdom), trees (growth), lamps (enlightenment).
- **Food**: Chef hats, forks, knives, leaves, wheat, bread, wine bottles.

If the symbol is a cliché, twist it:
- Combine it with an unexpected element (a rocket made of leaves = sustainable ambition).
- Change the perspective (a bird's-eye view of a tree instead of a side view).
- Use negative space to hide a secondary meaning (a coffee cup where the steam forms a brain).
- Abstract it until it becomes a unique geometric form (a leaf reduced to a single curved line).

## Symbol Selection

Select symbols that:
1. **Map to brand values**: The symbol must communicate something true about the brand.
2. **Have a strong silhouette**: Even in abstract form, the symbol should be recognizable.
3. **Simplify well**: The symbol must survive the icon simplification engine. If it becomes a blob at 16×16, it's not suitable as a primary symbol.
4. **Are culturally appropriate**: Avoid symbols with negative connotations in the target market's culture.
5. **Are legally safe**: Avoid symbols that are trademarked or too similar to existing marks in the category.

## Concept Diversity Requirements

When generating three concepts, ensure they are **significantly different**, not cosmetic variations. Each concept should differ in at least two of these dimensions:

- **Symbol**: Different primary symbol or metaphor.
- **Composition**: Different layout (e.g., centered vs. asymmetric, contained vs. open, vertical vs. horizontal).
- **Style**: Different style family or blend (e.g., Modern vs. Classic, Organic vs. Geometric).
- **Archetype emphasis**: Different archetype or different expression of the same archetype.
- **Metaphor**: Different visual metaphor for the same brand value.

### Example: Three Concepts for a Sustainable Adventure Brand

| Concept | Symbol | Composition | Style | Archetype | Metaphor |
|---------|--------|-------------|-------|-----------|----------|
| **Concept 1: The Summit** | Mountain peak | Centered, symmetrical, contained in a circle | Modern + Minimalist | Explorer | Achievement, reaching the peak |
| **Concept 2: The Current** | Flowing river + leaf | Asymmetric, diagonal, open | Organic + Nature | Explorer | Journey, natural flow, sustainability |
| **Concept 3: The Compass** | Abstract compass rose | Centered, geometric, contained in a hexagon | Geometric + Future | Explorer + Sage | Direction, exploration, wisdom |

Each concept uses a different symbol, different composition, and a different metaphor. They all fit the brand but express it differently.

## Composition Strategies

### Centered and Contained
- **Effect**: Stable, formal, balanced, institutional.
- **Best for**: Badges, emblems, traditional brands, Ruler archetype, Classic and Luxury styles.
- **Risk**: Can feel static or conservative. Avoid unless the brand needs authority.

### Centered and Open
- **Effect**: Balanced but approachable, modern, confident.
- **Best for**: Modern brands, logos with wordmarks, Innocent and Everyman archetypes.
- **Risk**: Can feel generic without a distinctive symbol.

### Asymmetric and Dynamic
- **Effect**: Energetic, progressive, contemporary, movement.
- **Best for**: Startups, tech, entertainment, Explorer and Creator archetypes.
- **Risk**: Can feel unstable or unbalanced if not carefully constructed. Use Visual Balance principle to compensate.

### Interlocking / Overlapping
- **Effect**: Connection, unity, integration, complexity.
- **Best for**: Partnerships, technology, community, Lover and Caregiver archetypes.
- **Risk**: Can become muddy at small sizes. Ensure overlapping forms are simple and high-contrast.

### Negative Space / Figure-Ground
- **Effect**: Intellectual, surprising, memorable, dual meaning.
- **Best for**: Brands that want to be perceived as clever, thoughtful, or innovative. Sage and Magician archetypes.
- **Risk**: Can be too subtle or confusing. Ensure both positive and negative shapes read clearly.

### Typography as Image
- **Effect**: Custom, ownable, typographic, editorial.
- **Best for**: Luxury, personal brands, fashion, publishing, monograms.
- **Risk**: Can be illegible if pushed too far. Ensure letterforms remain recognizable.

## Brand Storytelling Methods

A mark should tell a story, even if the story is simple. Methods:

1. **The origin story**: The mark represents how the brand began (e.g., a coffee brand using a ship because the founder was a sailor).
2. **The transformation**: The mark shows before and after, or a process (e.g., a caterpillar becoming a butterfly for a coaching brand).
3. **The hidden meaning**: The mark contains a secret that rewards the viewer who looks closer (e.g., FedEx arrow, Amazon A-to-Z smile).
4. **The values collage**: The mark combines multiple symbols that each represent a brand value (e.g., a shield + a heart + a leaf = protection + care + sustainability).
5. **The abstraction**: The mark is an abstracted form of something real, suggesting the brand without depicting it literally (e.g., an abstract wave for a surf brand).

Choose the method that best fits the brand's personality and audience. A Hero brand might use an origin story or transformation. A Sage brand might use hidden meaning or abstraction. A Jester brand might use visual puns or unexpected combinations.

## Concept Evaluation Quick Check

Before finalizing a concept, ask:
- [ ] Does this concept communicate the brand's core value in 1 second?
- [ ] Is the silhouette strong enough to recognize at 16×16?
- [ ] Does this concept feel different from competitors?
- [ ] Can I explain the symbolism in one sentence?
- [ ] Does the composition match the brand's archetype and style?
- [ ] Would this mark work in monochrome?
- [ ] Is the metaphor fresh, or is it a category cliché?
- [ ] Can this concept be simplified into a strong icon version?
